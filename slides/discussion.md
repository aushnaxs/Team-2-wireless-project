# DISCUSSION

Artificial intelligence is one of the most significant technologies shaping the future of wireless communication systems, given its ability to enhance network automation, resource management, prediction, and energy-consumption control. In 6G, where millions of devices are connected and communicating simultaneously, the traditional approach to network management is no longer effective. The use of artificial intelligence in 6G enables the network to make real-time decisions, predict network congestion, and control unnecessary resource consumption during off-peak hours. According to the use of artificial intelligence in 6G can enhance spectral efficiency and reduce operational waste in the network.


However, this advantage brings with it an interesting contradiction. Although AI helps networks save energy during operation, the AI networks themselves require considerable computational power for training, deployment, and continuous updating. Advanced machine learning networks require high-end GPUs, cloud servers, and data centres, which require considerable amounts of energy. Kamran et al. (2025) report that energy-aware 6G systems require intelligent resource allocation, adaptive power control, and energy-efficient architecture to improve long-term sustainability.

The role of AI in wireless communications is to improve system energy efficiency. This includes predictive scheduling, intelligent sleep modes, and adaptive resource management. AI systems can predict the demand for the network and put the unused components of the network to sleep. This saves energy as the base stations consume the most energy. However, despite the many advantages of AI, there are still important challenges associated with its large-scale deployment. These challenges go beyond just the energy required for electricity generation. They also include the use of rare earth materials, cooling systems, and the expansion of data centers needed to support AI hardware. In addition, AI systems often require continuous retraining to maintain performance under changing network conditions, which further increases resource consumption. In the context of telecom operators, this creates a trade-off: while operational efficiency improves, overall energy demand may also rise.


Another major concern is that future 6G networks will rely on ultra-dense infrastructure, including edge intelligence, distributed sensing, and semantic communication layers. These technologies require constant coordination and significant computational resources. Although AI enhances network efficiency and performance, it also introduces high computational costs. Training advanced machine learning models depends on high-performance processors, cloud computing systems, and repeated optimization cycles. As highlighted by Strubell, Ganesh, and McCallum (2022), repeated deep learning training can result in substantial carbon emissions, particularly when large-scale hardware clusters are involved.


This is where the sustainability paradox comes in. On one hand, AI is necessary to run future smart networks. On the other hand, if it keeps growing without limits, it could hurt environmental sustainability. The goal isn’t to stop using AI, but to use it more responsibly. That means designing systems where energy efficiency is built in from the start, not something added later.


At the end of the day, AI is going to be a huge part of future wireless networks. The research shows it can make systems faster and more efficient, but it also comes with real environmental costs. Looking across the literature, it’s clear both sides matter. If we only focus on performance and ignore sustainability, we could end up with networks that are advanced, but not truly sustainable in the long run.
